    NASA Programs

    I was a business major at University of Arkansas Pulaski Technical College when I received an email from my mathematics professor. It was an application for NASA Community College Aerospace Scholars (NCAS). I wasn't in STEM at the time, but I had enough technology electives that I qualified. I knew chances were slim, but I decided to risk it and see what would happen. I would rather be rejected than ask myself "what if" every time I laid my head down to sleep. I was accepted. The next five weeks consisted of tests and weekly meetings to compete for a slot at a NASA work site. This was in conjuction with working full-time, schooling full-time, and upholding the standard in my military career. Needless to say I got very little sleep, but I was never going to give in or quit. I knew this was an opportunity that would happen only once in my life, so I went full throttle to the finish line. After many sleepless nights I turned in my final project on the exercise equipment aboard the International Space Station and space suits. A few weeks later I became one of a few hundred students invited to attend a NASA work site for a week. I was elated and began gearing up to head to NASA Langley Research Center. With new found confidence I eagerly applied to the Lucy Student Pipeline Accelerator and Competency Enabler Mission Concept Academy (L'SPACE MCA), and changed my major to Computer Science.

    Then the Pandemic Hit...

    My trip to Langley Research Center was cancelled since all NASA worksites were closed to visitors. I was gutted. I decided to keep my eyes on the finish line, and went with a positive attitude into the L'SPACE program, which is named after the upcoming Lucy Mission to the Trojan Asteroids. L'SPACE consisted of nearly nightly ZOOM meetings as I collaborated with a team of STEM students in Chicago. We used Trello, Slack, Whatsapp, and Google Suite to collect and share our ideas. Because of my AS Business degree, and background in finance, I was unanimously voted in as the Lead Administrator. I was in charge of a $100 million dollar budget for Team 16's Preliminary Design Review (PDR). A PDR is essentially a sales pitch for a proposed NASA mission. I was able to work with the the Science and Engineer Leaders to keep spending low. We were able to come in underbudget with several million left over in margin. A large margin was considered great as long as we met all of our science goals, which we did. L'SPACE was a great experience and it was awesome to have virtual meetings with actual current NASA employees.
